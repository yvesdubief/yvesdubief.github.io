M2CE Lab

The Multiscale Mechanics and Computational Engineering Lab uses high fidelity simulations and computational models to investigate complex physics in fluid mechanics and heat transfer. It is where Elasto-Inertial Turbulence was first simulated and (re)-discovered. Research spans from turbulence theory to snow-farming.

People

PI: Yves Dubief

I am a faculty in the Department of Mechanical Engineering at the University of Vermont. My expertise is in fluid mechanics and numerical simulations of complex fluids in complex flows. My scientific career started with Prof. Bob Antonia at the University of Newcastle (NSW, Australia) where I gained a strong foundation in turbulence research, and a deep appreciation for experimental fluid mechanics. Fascinated by coherent structures, I joined Prof. Marcel Lesieur et Dr. Pierre Comte at the Laboratoire des Ecoulements Géophysiques et Industrielles (Université Grenoble Alpes, Grenoble, France) for my PhD to write a multi-domain numerical simulation code and study the dynamics of coherent structures in wall flows, with and without cavities. In 2001 I received a postdoctoral fellowship from the Center for Turbulence Research (Stanford University, CA, USA) under the supervision of Prof. Parviz Moin, which evolved in a research associate position from 2002 to 2005. During that time I led the numerical simulation team for the DARPA Friction Drag Reduction awarded to Stanford. Our group identified the mechanism of polymer and fiber drag reduction in connection with wall-turbulence's coherent structures for channel flows, conducted the first simulations in turbulent boundary layers, and develop a turbulence model capable of simulating high drag reduction regime. In 2005, I joined the Faculty of Mechanical Engineeering at University of Vermont. Since then I have been working on different projects, including sediment transport, blood coagulation, and the dynamics of lipid membranes under mechanical and nanoparticle stresses. The main focus has been on elasto inertial turbulence (EIT), a turbulent state caused by polymer additives, discovered in this lab in 2010. With the help of my collaborators we are pursuing the derivation of the theory of EIT, using high fidelity, multiscale numerical simulations. When I am not coding, analyzing data, probing equations or teaching, I like to enjoy all the seasons of Vermont (but winter is the favorite season) with my family and our dogs.

Research

Project 1: Elasto-Inertial Turbulence and Polymer Drag Reduction

Researchers: Fuqian Yin (ME PhD candidate), Ali Sharafati (ME PhD candidate), Pierre-Yves Goffin( ME PhD candidate, University of Liege), Joey del Toro (ME MS candidate)

The objective is to build the theory of EIT. Our effort is focused on the dynamics of coherent structures and how they drive statistics. Our research has established the following properties: existence of EIT in 3D flows, EIT exists in 2D parallel shear flows, EIT is driven by an inverse energy cascade, EIT has exact coherent states. Our approach focuses on careful high-fidelity simulations and a constant quest to understand how assumptions, approximations, and boundary conditions are affecting our results. Current projects included:

  • Investigation of the role of diffusion in polymer models for the simulation of EIT
  • Study of EIT in 2D Rayleigh-Bénard convection flows
  • Study of EIT in 2D channel flows
  • Study of EIT in 2D periodic flows
  • Investigation of EIT's small scales
  • Development of high-fidelity simulations for applications of EIT to propulsion and heat transfer problems

Project 2: Simulation of UVM Plasma-Torch

Researchers: Dillon Ryan ( ME MS candidate)

The UVM inductively-coupled plasma (ICP) torch is a unique facility designed to test heat shield materials for atmospheric entry of space vehicles. Our goal is to develop a simulation platform for gas/surface interactions including heat transfer, chemistry and fluid dynamics between the plasma jet of the ICP torch and heat shield samples.

Project 3: Heat Transfer and Sustainability

Researchers: Jay Montagna (ME BS candidate)

Heat transfer and sustainability are intimately connected. Our world revolves around conversion of energy into thermal energy as a source or a byproduct. Our current projects include the modeling of geothermal energy in the context of the UVM campus and the storage of snow of the summer to lower the carbon footprint of snow making for activities such as skiing

Project 4: Biolubrication

Researchers: Atena F (Bio-Eng. PhD)

This project explore the role of self-assembling phospholipids in the lubrication of synovial joints via molecular dynamics simulation.

EIT-PDR

Our research group has built unique expertise in the simulation of viscoelastic flows with the objective to explore how polymer and fiber additives can manipulate flow to decrease or increase turbulence, mixing, and heat transfer. Our simulations have been consistently at the forefront of the research in polymer drag reduction and have led to the (re-)discovery of elasto-inertial turbulence (EIT)

Polymer Drag Reduction

Toms (1949) discovered that dissolving minute amounts of polymers in a turbulent pipe flows could produce considerable reduction in friction drag (up to 80%). Our simulations enabled the derivation of the first conceptual model of the PDR mechanism. The model distinguished two phenomena. The first weakens vortices that cause turbulent friction drag (thus driving drag reduction). The polymer drag reduction physics is effectively a removal of flow energy by elastic energy through polymer stretching. The second consists of a reinjection of elastic energy into the flow. Identified in Dubief et al. (2004), this energy transfer from polymers to flow was then speculated to be responsible for the state of maximum drag reduction, the asymptotic of PDR that is not laminar and cannot be surpassed by increasing polymer concentration.

Elasto-Inertial Turbulence

In 2010, we leveraged faster computers than in the 2001-2005 era of PDR research to search for the dynamics behind the transfer of energy from polymers to flow. To our surprise, our simulations revealed a new set of coherent structures and a new state of turbulence, EIT. A recent literature review (Dubief, Terrapon, Hof, 2023) revealed that this state had been observed nearly a century ago, but had been forgotten by the scientific community. Since our first simulation of EIT, our research group has enjoyed great collaborations with University of Liege (Belgium), University of Cambridge (UK), University of Edinburgh (UK), Institute of Science and Technology (Austria), Monash University (Australia), Weizmann Institute of Science (Israel).
